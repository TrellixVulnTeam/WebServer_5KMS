For months, experts warned that our part of Idaho would be inundated by tens of thousands if not hundreds of thousands of visitors coming here to see the solar eclipse.

Where Were All The People?

As the weekend came, I noticed a little more traffic on the highway and on our city streets but nothing that could be considered multiples of what is typically there in August.

I’d been worried that when we drove to our acreage in Teton Valley on Sunday afternoon to set up camp where we would view the eclipse that we would hit heavy traffic. I checked the highway cameras and saw the traffic was benign so we decided to leave later than we had originally planned so we didn’t have to spend so much time sitting in an empty field waiting for it to get dark.

As we lay in our tent that night, there were a few more cars than usual that passed by on the dirt road. Perhaps five.

When the eclipse ended, given the apparent lack of traffic, I decided to abandon our plans to take the longer way back home and return via a shorter route that included a major highway.

Gridlock

All was well for the first 45 minutes, but then as we passed Rexburg on U.S. 20, I saw a line of vehicles at a near standstill. There was twenty-five miles of bumper to bumper traffic to Idaho Falls, and for my sons traveling to Utah the near certainty of hour upon hour of crawling along for hundreds of miles at less than twenty-five miles an hour.

I exited the highway before hitting the stopped traffic and took the back roads to our house, arriving perhaps thirty minutes later than normal.

My sons waited eight hours before venturing south from our home toward Utah. It took them ninety minutes to go thirty miles. I checked hotel prices near where they were; all sold out, except for three rooms available at a two-star hotel for $392. Instead, they called their aunt and spent the night there. They arose at 4AM and drove the rest of the way to Utah without incident.

Clearly, there had been thousands of visitors trickling into Idaho over a number of days leading up to the eclipse, but when it was over most decided to leave immediately, causing the gridlock.

Clearing Traffic and Markets

There is no market clearing price in a traffic crunch. The Idaho Department of Transportation doesn’t have a congestion pricing mechanism to charge travelers more when everyone wants to leave at once.

Instead, given enough time, the traffic eventually clears.

The financial markets don’t work that way. If a lot of investors decide to leave a particular asset class, market segment or ETF at the same time, prices fall.

Although there is a buyer for every seller, if the ferocity of investors trying to exit an asset class overwhelms the market makers and other buyers, prices can plummet.

Bond ETFs

One area of the market that institutional investors have been moving into but has yet to be tested if investors want to exit en masse is fixed income ETFs. The Wall Street Journal reported earlier this year that bond ETFs took in $92 billion of new fund flows in 2016, up 50% from 2015, and that pace of inflows has continued in 2017.

Block trades in which new bond ETF shares are created or redeemed in trading sizes of $200,000 or more now account for 25% of fixed income exchange traded product trades, according to Credit Suisse Group.

Jon Swaney, a portfolio manager and managing director at New York Life Investment Management, told the Wall Street Journal that “bond ETFs provide us the ability to get in and out of fixed-income markets very quickly—to dial up or down credit risk, or extend or shorten our duration.”

Swaney and other portfolio managers are able to get in and out of fixed income markets quickly because investors have yet to decide to leave at once as the Idaho eclipse tourists did.

Bond ETF share redemption via block trades involves the ETF sponsor giving its authorized participants a basket of bonds held by the ETF in exchange for ETF shares. The authorized participants or their clients then sells those bonds.

Liquidity Crunch?

Bonds don’t trade on exchanges like stocks but are sold through dealers. One reason institutional investors have been using bond ETFs more is because dealers have been less willing to hold inventory, which means bond trading has become less liquid, particularly for corporate issues, such as non-investment grade bonds.

In other words, the lack of liquidity in individual bonds has led investors to buy bond ETFs because they appear more liquid, but if there is a mass exodus from corporate bond ETFs, the prices of the underlying bonds and the ETFs could plummet and their yields spike.

Howard Marks, co-chairman of Oaktree Capital, wrote in a recent memo that “as a product of the last several years, ETFs’ promise of liquidity has yet to be tested in a major bear market, particularly in less-liquid fields like high yield bonds.”

Evolving Markets

The growing size of the bond ETF market is an example of how financial markets evolve and that evolution is one of the topics of next week’s podcast episode 170, which is based on a fascinating book by Andrew Lo titled “ Adaptive Markets: Financial Evolution at the Speed of Light.”

In particular, I’ll share how financial markets can be both efficient and inefficient depending on the behavior of investors.

Lo points out that “risk isn’t necessarily always rewarded—it depends on the environment. Investing in stocks for the long run may or may not be good advice, depending on where you’re investing, for how long, and your risk tolerance is. John Maynard Keynes famously said, ‘In the long run, we are all dead,” but it was a hedge fund manager who added the important coda, ‘Make sure the short run doesn’t kill you first.’”

Hopefully, individual investors’ portfolios won’t get killed if institutional investors decide to unload their bond ETFs at the same time.
